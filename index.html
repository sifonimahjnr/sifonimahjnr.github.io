Poetry

Spoken word
& reflection.

Dark, reflective, and written in the tradition of spoken word. These are not decorations. They are attempts at precision where prose falls short.

I.

Inheritance

My father gave me silence the way some fathers give their sons land as though stillness were an estate, and I should live off its yields. I learned to speak in semicolons. Sentences that hold but do not end. Thoughts that stand at thresholds and never quite go in. There is a grammar to grief that nobody teaches. You pick it up in waiting rooms, at three in the morning, in the space between a question and the answer that doesn't come. I am fluent now. I speak it like a first language.
II.

System Failure

The thing about breaking down is that you don't announce it. No error message, no stack trace. Just a slow degradation masked as function. I have run at forty percent and called it fine. Kept logs I never read. Flagged warnings I patched without fixing the root. The human body has no rollback. There is no restore point before the damage. You patch forward. You always patch forward.
III.

Cartography

I have been drawing a map of things I do not understand. The territory keeps changing. I keep drawing. Not because understanding is near but because the act of mapping is the only honest prayer I know how to make. Every question is a coordinate. Every answer, a revision. The map is never finished. That is not a failure. That is the work.
IV.

On Disappearing

There are people who leave quietly so quietly you mistake it for them still being there. Their silences become furniture. You arrange your life around them. You stop noticing. And then one day the room looks different and you can't name what's gone only that it is and that you miss it in the dull ache way of muscle memory reaching for something that has already been moved.

experiments.

Short but serious. Startup hypotheses, thought experiments, observations not yet ready to become essays. Some will go nowhere. That is their right.

Startup Concept
A tool that maps your knowledge gaps — not your knowledge

Every learning platform maps what you know. None map what you believe you understand but don't. A diagnostic system that surfaces false confidence is worth more than any flashcard app. The hardest thing to learn is that you're wrong about something you thought you understood.

edtechepistemologytooling
Thought Experiment
What if security was measured by what you give up, not what you protect?

We measure security by what we lock down. But every lock is a constraint on movement. The most interesting security question is not "what cannot enter?" but "what can no longer leave?" Threat models that account for operational friction would look very different from the ones we build.

cybersecurityphilosophy
Observation
The most dangerous software is software that almost works

Broken software fails visibly. Software that almost works produces confident, coherent, and wrong outputs. This is true of large language models. It is also true of legislation, economic models, and people. We underinvest in the failure modes of near-correct systems.

Startup Concept
A structured notebook for ideas with decay timers

Most ideas should die. The problem is we keep them alive in flat note-taking systems with no pressure to act or discard. An idea vault with configurable TTLs — after which entries either become commitments or are archived — would force the decision most people avoid.

Observation
Attention has a gravity that expertise cannot override

Even people who understand algorithmic distraction remain susceptible to it. Knowledge of a system is not immunity from that system. This is relevant for cybersecurity professionals, psychologists, and anyone who believes that understanding a bias neutralises it.

Short-form. Unfinished. Things noticed that seemed too small for an essay and too significant to lose.

The best mental models are the ones you forget. If you have to remember to apply a framework, it hasn't become thought yet — it's still a tool sitting outside your mind, waiting to be picked up. Real understanding is transparent. You think through it without thinking about it.

on mental models
"Simplicity is a great virtue but it requires hard work to achieve it and education to appreciate it. And to make matters worse: complexity sells better."
— Edsger W. Dijkstra

Every system has a grammar. Programming languages have syntax. Networks have protocols. Organisations have culture. The fastest way to understand something is to learn its grammar — not the rules, but the assumptions the rules are built on. The rules change. The assumptions are more durable.

"I have no special talent. I am only passionately curious."

There is a difference between writing to communicate and writing to think. Most writing advice applies to the first. Almost none of it applies to the second. The notebook is not an audience. It is a laboratory. The standard for clarity there is different — you need only to be clear enough to return to an idea and continue it.

on writing

Security is not a state, it is a posture. You are never secure — you are only more or less difficult to compromise at a given moment. The mistake is treating it as a destination. It is a practice, and the practice never ends.

About

On the
purpose of this.

"A person who has not made peace with his losses will often try to borrow their power from whatever is around them." — This site is an attempt at ownership.

My name is Sifon Imahjnr. I am studying software engineering and cybersecurity — not because these fields are lucrative, though they are, but because they are the closest I have found to a discipline of thinking clearly under constraint.

This website is an archive. The word is deliberate. Archives are not optimised for consumption. They are built for preservation and return — for the possibility of coming back to something years later and finding it still there, still useful, still honest.

I write essays when an idea requires more than a paragraph. I write poetry when it requires less than arguments can carry. I take notes when I encounter something I do not want to lose to the speed of forgetting. I build things when thinking alone is insufficient.

None of this is public performance. It is a private practice made visible — partly to hold myself accountable to the work, and partly on the small chance that something here is useful to someone who finds it.

If you have found something here that matters to you, or that you want to argue with, you are welcome to reach out.
